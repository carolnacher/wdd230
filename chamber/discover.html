Uruguay, located in South America, has an area of ​​176,220 km2. Uruguay, with a population of 3,426,260 people, is in position 134 in the population table, made up of 196 countries and maintains a very low population density, 19 inhabitants per km2. Its capital is Montevideo and its currency is Uruguayan pesos.

Uruguay is the 82nd economy by GDP volume. Its public debt in 2022 was 40,058 million euros, with a debt of 59.31% of GDP. Its per capita debt is €11,691 euros per inhabitant. The last annual variation rate of the CPI published in Uruguay is from January 2024 and was 5.1%. There are some variables that can help you learn more if you are traveling to Uruguay or simply want to know more about the standard of living of its inhabitants. GDP per capita is a very good indicator of the standard of living and in the case of Uruguay, in 2022, it was €19,712 euros, which is why it is in 49th place out of the 196 countries in the GDP per capita ranking. Regarding the Human Development Index or HDI, which is prepared by the United Nations to measure the progress of a country and which ultimately shows us the standard of living of its inhabitants, it indicates that Uruguayans are in 59th place. If the reason for visiting Uruguay is business, it is useful to know that Uruguay is in 101st place out of the 190 that make up the Doing Business ranking, which classifies countries according to the ease they offer for doing business.

Regarding the Corruption Perception Index of the public sector in Uruguay, it has been 74 points, therefore, its inhabitants have a low level of perception of government corruption. Carnival is the largest popular festival in our country. In Uruguay it has been celebrated since colonial times, having transformed from the traditional “barbarian festival” with assaults and street guerrillas characteristic of the 19th century, to the “civilized” festival of parades and neighborhood tablados consolidated in the 20th century and up to the present day. In Montevideo this festival opens with a parade that takes place along 18 de Julio Avenue, in which the main carnival groups participate (parodists, murgas, comedians, magazines and troupes of blacks and lubolos), which are part of the Official Contest .

Two weeks later, the biggest festival of the Afro-Uruguayan community takes place: the Llamadas Parade, where the protagonists are dozens of troupes that play and dance to the rhythm of candombe, while they walk down Isla de Flores street between the Sur and Palermo neighborhoods.

During its first decades, carnivals in Uruguay were celebrated with costume balls, water games, horseback riding through the city, without the presence of the Afro-descendant community. The Comparsas or Black and Lubolos Societies began to join the Carnival parades in the 1870s, but only at the beginning of the 20th century incorporating the practice of candombe into their participation. Throughout the 20th century, the troupes were consolidated in relation to the Montevideo carnival festivities, also participating in its contest and tablados.

In their beginnings, the Carnival parades covered a long route: they started from Plaza Constitución, traveled through several streets of the Old City, passed through 18 de Julio, reached Plaza Cagancha and returned to the starting point for another route. Technological advances brought lighting, first gas and then electricity, which adorned the avenues with more splendor each year. Between the end of the 19th century and the beginning of the 20th, our Carnival became one of the longest in the world, lasting for forty days. Throughout February and part of March the groups perform on neighborhood stages called tablados, and in the official competition at the Ramón Collazo Summer Theater. Along with the central and traditional activities of the Official Contest, Inaugural Parade, Parade of Calls and tablados and neighborhood parades, in recent decades other spaces of great participation have been consolidated around the carnival festivities: The Samba Schools Parade, held on Av. 18 de Julio the day after the Inaugural Parade. The Carnival of Promises, which in the 5 categories of the carnival brings together girls, boys and adolescents from various neighborhoods of the capital, participating in various instances that culminate in the Official Meeting of Groups at the Summer Theater during the months of December and January. The Murga Joven meeting, which brings together dozens of young people between 15 and 35 years old, who are part of murga collectives that are presented in two instances, after developing a process of training and collective creation through the artistic disciplines that the Murga develops as a popular and traditional artistic genre of Montevideo culture.
